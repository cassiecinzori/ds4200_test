Design Notes
Project: Mapping Urban Change Through 311 Service Requests

Published website link:
https://iasolb.github.io/DS4200_GroupProject/

General design idea:
We wanted the website to feel like one connected story about how Boston 311 patterns changed from 2015 to 2025. The figures go from a broad overview, to neighborhood comparisons, to change over time, and then to tract-level validation. We kept the webpage style simple so the figures stand out and feel consistent.

Figure 1: Seasonal Patterns in 311 Requests
This heatmap is the overview figure. We used a grid because it makes monthly patterns easy to compare, especially winter-related request types.

Figure 2: Neighborhood Request Composition Analysis
We used horizontal grouped bars because the request type names are long and because the main goal is comparing 2015 and 2025 side by side.

Figure 3: Neighborhood Signature Drift Quantification
This figure is meant to show which neighborhoods changed the most. Sorting the values makes the ranking easier to read, and the reference line helps show what counts as above-average drift.

Figure 4: Cluster Distribution Comparison
This figure shows how neighborhoods group together based on similar complaint patterns. Different colors make the clusters easier to separate, and the stable/shifted markers help show change across years.

Figure 5: Interactive Neighborhood Request Pattern Comparison
This figure is for closer inspection of one neighborhood at a time. The dropdown lets the viewer focus on a specific neighborhood, and the legend interaction helps compare years more easily.

Figure 6: Tract-Level Socioeconomic Validation
This D3 scatter plot was made to compare tract poverty rates with complaint outcomes. We kept the interaction simple: the dropdown changes the y-axis metric, hover shows tract details, and clicking a point pins it. Point size shows population and point color shows income.

Figure 7: Interactive Geographic Visualization
The map is meant to show where requests are located across the city. Side-by-side maps make the two years easier to compare, and the filters help narrow the view to a neighborhood or request type.

How the figures connect:
The figures are not meant to be separate. Figure 1 gives the general timing patterns, Figures 2 to 4 explain neighborhood-level differences and change, Figure 5 lets the user explore specific neighborhoods, Figure 6 checks whether the story also appears at the tract level, and Figure 7 brings the analysis back to geography.
