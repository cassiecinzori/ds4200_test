Mapping Urban Change Through 311 Service Requests

A Comparative Analysis of Boston Neighborhoods: 2015 vs 2025

Anthony Campos Cassandra Cinzori Ian Solberg

Introduction

This study examines how Boston neighborhoods transformed between 2015 and 2025 through the lens of municipal 311 service request patterns. Municipal 311 systems provide a comprehensive record of non-emergency issues reported by residents, including infrastructure maintenance requests, code violations, and quality-of-life complaints. The georeferenced and timestamped nature of these requests enables granular analysis of neighborhood-level conditions and concerns.

Prior research demonstrates that 311 request patterns serve as reliable indicators of local socioeconomic context. Wang et al. (2017) established that clustering neighborhoods by request-type frequencies produces groups with distinctly different income, education, and demographic profiles. By comparing these "signatures" across a decade, we identify neighborhoods experiencing significant transformation through gentrification, infrastructure investment, or emerging distress patterns.

The 2015-2025 period represents a transformative decade for Boston, characterized by substantial Seaport District development, accelerated gentrification in historically lower-income neighborhoods, and increased climate-related infrastructure challenges. This analysis quantifies which neighborhoods changed most dramatically and interprets the implications for Boston's urban landscape.

Data

Our analysis utilizes Boston's publicly available 311 service request records from the City of Boston Open Data portal. Each record contains case metadata, geographic coordinates, timestamps, and neighborhood classifications. We analyze two complete calendar years separated by a decade: 2015 and 2025.

Data Processing

Both datasets were filtered to closed cases only. We excluded request categories with limited relevance to neighborhood condition assessment, including parking enforcement, routine recycling, street cleaning, graffiti removal, animal control, abandoned vehicle enforcement, and traffic signal maintenance. This filtering process yields 118,429 records for 2015 and 107,778 records for 2025.

The 11% reduction in filtered requests over the decade, despite population growth, merits consideration as a potential finding rather than a data quality concern. This trend may reflect improved municipal responsiveness reducing repeat complaints, evolving civic engagement patterns, or migration toward alternative digital reporting channels.

Supplementary Data

We integrated American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year estimates (2019-2023) providing census tract-level data on population, median household income, and poverty rates. Geographic assignment of 311 requests to census tracts via spatial join enables validation of request patterns against demographic indicators. This approach follows the methodology established by Wang et al. (2017) for linking 311 data to socioeconomic context.

Methodology

Following Wang et al. (2017), we construct a "311 pattern signature" for each neighborhood, defined as a normalized vector representing the relative frequency distribution of request types. This normalization enables meaningful comparison across neighborhoods regardless of absolute request volume, as the signature captures the compositional structure rather than scale.

Signature Construction

For neighborhood a with request types t, the signature vector S(a) is defined as S(a) = (s(a,t) / s(a)) where s(a,t) represents the count of type t requests in area a and s(a) equals the total requests in a. Each signature vector sums to 1.0, representing a probability distribution over request types.

Clustering Analysis

We apply k-means clustering with k=4 to group neighborhoods exhibiting similar request patterns. The choice of four clusters follows Wang et al.'s methodology for Boston and reflects evaluation of within-cluster variance and interpretability. Clustering is performed independently for each year to identify structural changes in neighborhood groupings.

Change Quantification

Cosine distance measures the angular separation between neighborhood signature vectors across years. This metric quantifies compositional change independent of magnitude, with values approaching 1.0 indicating substantial pattern transformation. Neighborhoods exhibiting high cosine distance experienced significant shifts in their request type distribution, potentially reflecting demographic transitions, infrastructure investment patterns, or evolving service needs.

Findings

Neighborhood-Level Transformation

East Boston, South Boston/Waterfront, and South Boston demonstrate the most substantial signature drift values, indicating dramatic shifts in request pattern composition. These findings align with documented rapid development and gentrification in these areas during the study period. The Seaport District's transformation from industrial zone to high-density mixed-use development is clearly reflected in evolving complaint patterns.

Request Volume Trajectory

The 11% reduction in filtered 311 requests despite population growth warrants interpretation beyond data quality concerns. This trend may indicate improved municipal service delivery efficiency reducing duplicate complaints, shifting civic engagement modalities, or adoption of alternative digital reporting platforms. Further investigation of response time metrics and mobile application adoption rates could clarify these dynamics.

Temporal Stability of Core Services

Seasonal patterns in infrastructure-related requests demonstrate remarkable consistency across the decade. Snow removal requests maintain winter concentration in both years, while year-round concerns such as pothole repair and street lighting show stable temporal distributions. This consistency suggests that fundamental municipal service demands persist despite neighborhood-level transformations.

Cluster Reorganization

Redistribution of neighborhoods across cluster assignments between 2015 and 2025 indicates systematic reorganization of Boston's service request profile landscape. This restructuring likely reflects broader demographic and economic transitions occurring citywide, with implications for resource allocation and service delivery planning.
